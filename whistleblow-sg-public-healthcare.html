Section 1. Dangerous shift hours

One of the major factors that contributed to my decision to leave the public healthcare system in Singapore was the alarming practice of subjecting junior doctors to grueling 30-hour shifts, commonly known as "Full-calls." These shifts typically lasted from 6:30 AM on the first day until 12:30 PM or 1:00 PM the following day, leaving minimal opportunities for adequate rest and recovery.

The risks associated with sleep deprivation, particularly in the healthcare industry, cannot be overstated. Numerous studies have shown that being awake for extended periods can impair cognitive function to levels comparable to alcohol intoxication. For instance, being awake for 24 hours is similar to having a blood alcohol concentration (BAC) of 0.10%, exceeding Singapore's legal drink-driving limit of 0.08%.

During these extended shifts, the chances of getting quality sleep were slim to none. Out of the approximately 35 full-calls I experienced, the best shift granted me only 3 hours of continuous sleep – an extreme rarity. Unsurprisingly, morale among junior doctors, including house officers, registrars, and those subjected to these full-calls, was quite low.

Alarmingly, at any given time in Singapore's public hospitals, a significant proportion of doctors were making critical decisions regarding patients' lives and treatment plans while operating under cognitive impairment levels akin to alcohol intoxication.

Furthermore, chronic sleep deprivation is known to increase the risk of cardiovascular disease and certain cancers. Ironically, healthcare workers subjected to these conditions may contribute more than the average person to Singapore's disease burden in their later years due to the occupational hazards they endure.

1.1 Normalisation of dangerous practices

What baffled me the most was the stark contrast in perspectives between colleagues who had trained or worked overseas and those who had not. For my colleagues who never ventured beyond Singapore's healthcare system, the abnormal had become the norm. It boggled my mind when they believed that hospitals functioning in such a manner was considered normal practice. The unhealthy work culture and unsafe conditions were so ingrained that they failed to recognize the need for change.

The situation escalated to the point where some doctors began calling in sick on the days they were scheduled for full-calls, leading to the implementation of backup on-call doctors as part of the regular schedule. Disturbingly, even the first-level backup on-call doctors began calling in sick, necessitating the involvement of second-level backups.

It is crucial to acknowledge that junior doctors often serve as the first point of contact for patients in public hospitals, making initial decisions about investigations and treatments before a consultant reviews the case. Any mistakes made under the influence of sleep deprivation could have severe consequences for patient care.

1.2 Lack of professional courtesy

Alarmingly, the hospital often failed to provide courtesy or advance warning to the backup doctors about their overnight duty. I vividly remember an incident where I unexpectedly found my name on the roster for an overnight shift, with no prior notification. This lack of consideration and communication from the hospital was unacceptable and deeply concerning.

Ultimately, I could not reconcile myself with the dysfunctional work culture and unsafe practices that not only jeopardized the health and well-being of healthcare staff but also put patients at risk. This realization, coupled with the inefficiencies in administrative tasks and outdated medical record systems, led me to the difficult decision to leave the system.

Section 2. Toxic work culture

Another significant factor that contributed to my decision to leave the public healthcare system in Singapore was the lack of support, open communication, and accountability within the organizational culture.

During my time working as a doctor in Australia, I experienced a collaborative and supportive environment. When faced with challenges, such as working late due to a registrar's inefficient patient rounds, the nursing staff would lodge formal complaints on my behalf, recognizing the strain I was under. Superiors were open to answering questions and even adjusting management plans based on input from junior doctors.

However, this supportive atmosphere was sorely missing in Singapore's public health system. Instead, a rigid hierarchy prevailed, characterized by blame-shifting and a reluctance to speak up due to fear of retaliation. Junior doctors who made decisions deviating from a consultant's plan, even after grueling 36-hour shifts, were often subjected to berating phone calls from the primary care team, not for educational purposes but solely to chastise them without explaining the rationale behind the consultant's approach.

This toxic environment led to a common practice among medical officers, registrars, and even house officers – maintaining separate phone subscriptions to disconnect from work during off-hours, avoiding unnecessary and demoralizing reprimands.

Disturbingly, these reprimands were frequently not conducted by the consultants themselves but carried out by their subordinates under instructions, or by individuals who had been berated and then asked to convey the same treatment to their colleagues who were already off-duty. This indirect and cowardly approach to communication was unprofessional and detrimental to fostering a healthy work environment.

Consequently, staff morale suffered as individuals became defensive and fearful of making mistakes. Only the consultants possessed a comprehensive understanding of the rationale behind investigations and interventions for patients, while others had fragmented information. Patients and their families were often left in the dark, unable to fully comprehend or comment on treatment plans unless they had inside knowledge of the system.

This lack of open communication, support, and accountability within the healthcare system hindered the delivery of quality patient care and fostered an unhealthy work environment. It became evident that leaving the system was necessary to preserve my professional integrity and well-being.

This breakdown in communication and the pervasive culture of fear hindered the delivery of quality patient care. Nursing staff, junior doctors, and even patients' families felt unable to voice their concerns or advocate for appropriate care due to the risk of repercussions from higher authorities. Ultimately, this lack of open dialogue and accountability within the healthcare system fostered an unhealthy work environment and compromised the well-being of both patients and staff members.

2.1 Hierarchical Culture: stifles collaboration

I argue that electronic medical records (EMRs) prioritize medicolegal defense over ensuring effective transfer of patient information. Much useful data about a patient's care, such as family dynamics, personality traits, and strategies to improve treatment adherence, are often omitted from records due to concerns over political correctness.

Nurses and junior doctors appear reluctant to contradict consultants or voice differing opinions, even when advocating for family wishes. This hierarchical culture impedes open collaboration and negatively impacts patient care, a persistent issue since I last worked in the system.

Crucially, junior doctors do not receive adequate explanations for senior doctors' decisions, hindering their ability to develop their own clinical judgment and determine the kind of doctor they aspire to become.

The lack of interdisciplinary collaboration and transparent communication ultimately undermines patient care within the public healthcare system.

2.2 Disempowerment of Junior Staff and Allied Health Professionals

While hierarchical structures within hospitals can provide workflow guidance and clarity around accountability, an overly rigid hierarchy can be detrimental, particularly when it inhibits open communication and exchange of ideas across roles. Unfortunately, this toxic dynamic plagues the Singapore public healthcare system, where communication is predominantly top-down.

Allied health professionals and junior staff often feel disempowered to voice their perspectives, opinions, or provide input that may contradict or challenge the consultants' directives. This one-way communication flow stifles valuable insights from those closest to patient care.

Rather than fostering a collaborative environment that leverages the diverse expertise across disciplines, the entrenched hierarchical culture has created an environment where junior staff and allied health professionals are expected to simply execute orders without question or discourse.

This lack of empowerment not only hinders professional growth and development but also raises concerns about the quality of patient care. When frontline providers cannot freely contribute their unique perspectives and lived experiences, opportunities for optimizing treatment plans and addressing patient needs may be overlooked.

Ultimately, the Singapore public healthcare system's rigid hierarchical culture impedes the free flow of ideas, stymies interprofessional collaboration, and disempowers those closest to patient care, potentially compromising the quality and comprehensiveness of care delivered.

Section 3. Breach of Medical ethics

The 4 pillars of medical ethics are:

  • Beneficence (doing good)
  • Non-maleficence (to do no harm)
  • Autonomy (giving the patient the freedom to choose freely, where they are able)
  • Justice (ensuring fairness)

3.1 Prioritizing Patient-Centered Care Over Rigid Protocol Adherence

While protocols offer benefits like streamlined processes and reduced cognitive load, an overreliance on them can impede patient-centered care. Medicine is an intrinsically human endeavor, dealing with unique individuals whose needs and responses to treatment may vary. Rigidly adhering to protocols without considering each patient's physiology, goals, and circumstances undermines truly personalized care.

Common justifications like "it's the institution's protocol" fail to provide valid clinical rationale. Protocols should serve as reference points, not inviolable rules. In the era of artificial intelligence, doctors who rigidly adhere to guidelines without accounting for individual patient circumstances are no better than AI systems blindly following protocols designed for idealized scenarios divorced from real-world practice's complexities. True empathetic clinicians, however, cannot be replaced by AI, as they possess the ability to tailor care to the complexities and nuances of real-world practice, going beyond mere guideline implementation.

I frequently find myself questioning the reasoning behind certain protocol-driven actions that seem disconnected from patient needs. For instance, why require an ECG trace to determine if a patient is dead? Isn't the absence of breathing, heart sounds/central pulse for more than 5 minutes, absence of pupillary response to light, no response to touch/voice/pain (central painful stimulus), coupled with the obvious sign of the body becoming ambient temperature, more than enough to certify death? Requiring ECG confirmation when other definitive signs of death are already present seems unnecessary.

Guidelines aim to reduce cognitive load for providers, but their judicious application requires incorporating the patient's and family's perspectives along with clinical judgement, rather than blindly following protocols divorced from real-world scenarios.

Truly patient-centered care necessitates striking a balance - utilizing protocols as helpful frameworks while maintaining the flexibility to tailor management plans to each person's unique situation. An excessive institutional emphasis on unwavering protocol adherence compromises our ability to humanize healthcare delivery.
