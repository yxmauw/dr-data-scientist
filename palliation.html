Palliation with active TB - It does not exist

The hospital admission

Got the news one Thursday evening that my grandmother was admitted to a public hospital in Singapore. Oh, it was triggered by carer's fatigue, but posed as possible lung infection to instil perceived severe condition to get priority. (well, she did have lung infection, so it is not entirely wrong) My mother (carer) was not clear with the goals of care, so the doctors covered their bases. I got mad about the interventions done, so I took over the decision-making. Worse luck, she could not be discharged to institutional care because she might have active pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB). So far, it has been a prolonged journey, and the end is not yet reached. Emotions have been on a roller-coaster ride. Here are my thoughts so far (and I mean no disrespect to Drs in Singapore, I was 1 of you too):

  1. Talking to nursing staff and junior doctors is useless if wanna change anything in management plans. Need to speak to the consultant.
  2. Can only speak to consultant during ward round. If u r lucky. Best luck is between 9 and 9.30am
  3. Consultants are rotated every week to cover isolation ward
    4. li>
  4. Isolation room ambient temperature is customisable. So it doesn't have to be freezing for little old ladies
  5. It is ok not to measure BP in someone who is dying and goals of care does not include resuscitative measures - be confident in using presence of pulse and breathing, pupillary response to light, and response to pain/voice/touch as signs of life for monitoring.
  6. Use Oxygen supplementation for absolute hypoxaemia. It does not help with distress from dyspnoea in end of life. That needs something else.
  7. There is no room in Singapore for palliation in presumed active TB patients. All need to undergo treatment even if goals of care is comfort only.
  8. Nurses and junior drs seem to fear going against consultants. Or even to voice a different opinion or to advocate for family wishes. That hasn't changed since I last left the system. Quite unfortunate. Patients are the ones left to suffer.
  9. Asking nurses and junior staff for rationale behind management decisions is useless.
  10. Why is the treatment 9 months instead of 6 months?
  11. Why do you need to keep measuring BP in someone whose BP you are not going to treat?
  12. Why do you need to subject frail old lady to a CT scan in search for overt internal bleed if cannot fix the source of bleeding for a non-surgical candidate?
  13. Why do you need to check response to platelet transfusion if you know she cannot tolerate a repeat transfusion?
  14. Junior drs don't learn the rationale behind decisions made by senior drs, which would help them in forming their internal compass about what kind of Dr they would want to be.
  15. You don't need an ECG to confirm biological death
  16. There should be an option for Euthanasia for cases like this
  17. I had to choose the path of accelerated death for my grandmother due to iatrogenic cause. Which is TB treatment regime that are toxic to end-organs (eyes, liver, kidneys). She had no functional reserve left in kidneys prior to treatment
  18. My grandmother probably will die with TB, instead of TB. Cause of death would be advanced dementia, with multi-organ failure secondary to TB treatment toxicity
  19. There is no option to discharge untreated presumed active TB into institutional care even if patient is frail and death is imminent within 6 months.
  20. Singapore public healthcare system is protocol-driven rather than patient-centred
  21. Most Doctors here have poor communication skills
  22. Most Doctors here are very reticent to share information regarding patient's care unless pushed by patient/family to answer targeted questions
  23. Open-ended questions to most Doctors here do not yield useful answers, you got to know your s*t if you ever get to ask them anything
  24. The common cop-out reason given for decision rationale is because it is the "institution" protocol. Tip to public: that is not a valid rationale, because all decisions can be altered if the situation is deemed suitable or change requested.
  25. Another tip: protocols/guidelines are first point of reference for Drs to use to reduce cognitive load. But what they do with the reference is entirely their choice, be it likened to a machine learning algorithm, following everything by the book without considering the patient's/family's POV or being an empathetic clinician that cannot be replaced by artificial intelligence.
  26. Treat the patient, not the numbers
  27. Guidelines cover standard and/or ideal situations. When is medicine practice ever ideal or involve dealing with "standardised" patients?
