Section 1. The hospital admission ordeal

One Thursday evening, I received news that my grandmother had been admitted to a public hospital in Singapore. The stated trigger was a potential lung infection, though later it seemed this was exaggerated due to caregiver burnout, aiming to prioritize her admission.

From the outset, there was a lack of clarity around the goals of care. Practicing defensive medicine, the doctors proceeded with interventions that I strongly disagreed with. This prompted me to take over decision-making for my grandmother.

The situation took an unexpected turn when concerns arose about her potentially having active pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB), complicating plans for her discharge to an institutional care facility.

What ensued was a prolonged and emotionally turbulent journey that finally concluded on May 18th, 2024. Throughout this arduous experience, I grappled with a range of thoughts and sentiments:

1.1 The Harsh Reality: Advocating for Personalized Care Requires Direct Physician Engagement

If I wanted any changes made to my grandmother's treatment plan, interventions or monitoring, I realized the harsh truth - no nurse, allied health professional or junior doctor was truly going to advocate on our behalf. They seemed too fearful of offending the consultants, as their performance reviews likely depended on staying in their good graces.

My best chance to have our voices heard was to catch the consultant directly during ward rounds. Those typically happened early morning around 7:30am and could drag on until lunch depending on caseload. I learned the isolation ward's prime window was 9-9:30am.

I tried calling the hospital hotline to at least reach someone on the medical team, but that proved useless. I only ever got through to a nurse providing scripted, patronizing responses about protocol rather than facilitating any actual communication with the doctors.

It became abundantly clear that if we wanted personalized care accounting for my grandmother's needs and our family's preferences, I would have to persistently pursue face-to-face opportunities to directly engage the consulting physician. Relying on other staff to properly convey our perspectives seemed futile. The onus was on me to forcefully insert our voices into her care process.

1.2 Fragmented Care: Rotating Consultants and the Breakdown of Continuity

In the isolation ward, consultants rotate on a weekly basis, likely an attempt to minimize staff exposure and infection risk. While the electronic medical record (EMR) system appears to facilitate the transfer of pertinent case details across teams, communication breaks down when aspects fall outside the realm of "protocol."

The primary purpose of EMRs seems to be ensuring medicolegal protection for staff rather than seamlessly transitioning care to provide the best possible treatment for the patient. Details recorded must remain "politically correct," lest the hospital throw providers "under the bus" in litigations.

From my perspective as a trained physician, this practice of frequently rotating doctors deprives junior medical trainees of invaluable opportunities to witness and learn from longitudinal patient cases firsthand. While they can study case histories for presentations, nothing compares to directly interacting with patients and families throughout their healthcare journey.

In this system, junior doctors rotate alongside the consultants they are assigned to, following whichever new cases are allocated to that attending physician. This disrupts continuity of care and hinders their professional development.

My personal growth as a clinician was profoundly shaped by experiences following patients over extended periods. It provided a holistic understanding of what constitutes truly excellent medical practice – something the current staffing model may be failing to impart.

1.3 Panic over expected death

Hospital protocols, while generally helpful, can become a significant impediment when it comes to respecting family members' wishes for their loved one's end-of-life experience. Regular monitoring of vital signs like temperature, pulse, respiration, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation is standard practice for assessing treatment response. However, for patients like my grandmother, whose goals shift from curative to comfort-focused care, adhering rigidly to such protocols proves counterproductive.

My grandmother was cachexic and in stage 5 renal failure, ineligible for dialysis, transplantation, or invasive procedures. Initially, due to unclear communication of her wishes, the medical team treated her with curative intent. This led to a barrage of tests - blood work, imaging, and even a CT scan to investigate internal bleeding despite her unsuitability for surgical intervention.

Tests found incidental anaemia and thrombocytaemia which led to more interventions. She underwent a platelet transfusion that promptly crashed her blood pressure, necessitating high-risk fluid resuscitation that her compromised renal function could scarcely tolerate. The rationale for rechecking her platelet levels post-transfusion seemed questionable given her inability to undergo further transfusions safely.

This cascade of interventions proved highly distressing for my fragile grandmother, leaving her plagued by nightmares and chilled despite double blankets - a far cry from a peaceful end-of-life experience. I couldn't fathom the reasoning behind these seemingly unnecessary investigations and lack of satisfactory explanations from clinical staff to my mother.

Ultimately, I intervened to directly communicate with the medical team, aiming to establish clearer accountability and ensure care aligned with my grandmother's needs and our family's wishes during her final days.

1.4 Humanizing End-of-Life Care: Moving Beyond Rigid Protocols

When a patient is actively dying and the goals of care shift to palliation, adhering blindly to protocols that mandate measuring vital signs serves little purpose beyond creating unnecessary distress. For my grandmother, whose peripheral circulation was shutting down, obsessively pursuing blood pressure readings and oxygen saturation levels added no value to her care.

While remote monitoring can be reasonable during periods of reduced bedside presence, I struggled to comprehend the fixation on obtaining blood pressure readings when it was glaringly apparent she was nearing death's doorstep. This misguided focus culminated in a distressing incident where nurses, alarmed by her immeasurable pulse on standard equipment, wheeled in a crash cart despite my grandmother's obvious breathing pattern.

Their panic seemed to stem from an excessive reliance on numeric vital signs rather than applying clinical judgement – neglecting the fundamental principle that breathing precedes pulse assessment for initiating life-saving measures. Worse, they disregarded my explicit wishes against uncomfortable procedures and were dishonest about attempting blood pressure measurements.

Throughout this experience, I continuously encountered the frustrating justification of "it's protocol" from staff, suggesting an alarmingly rigid adherence to standardized processes without thoughtful consideration for each patient's unique condition and circumstances. This robotic approach undermines the very essence of medical training by discounting critical thinking and clinical reasoning in favor of blindly following directives akin to an artificial intelligence system simply executing programmed rules.

Another concerning protocol was the requirement for ECG confirmation before certifying death, causing unnecessary anguish for families witnessing their loved ones growing cold while awaiting the arbitrary designation of asystole. As a physician, I've been on the other side of this devastating experience, struggling to provide closure to distraught families based solely on bureaucratic criteria divorced from human compassion.

Truly humanizing end-of-life care necessitates transcending inflexible protocols that prioritize bureaucracy over dignity. It requires healthcare professionals to apply clinical acumen, empathy, and ethical judgment when established guidelines conflict with a patient's circumstantial needs and a family's wishes during this sacred transition.

1.5 Forcing Suffering: When Palliative Care Isn't Enough

The experience with my grandmother's end-of-life care was truly heart-wrenching. She suffered from advanced dementia, and caring for her at home had become unmanageable for my mother. We simply wanted to transfer her to a long-term care facility. However, an upper lobe cavitating lung lesion raised concerns about her potentially having pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB).

No nursing home or long-term care facility could accept someone with suspected active TB and not undergo TB treatment, even if death was imminent within 6 months. Home hospice wasn't an option either if we refused the TB treatment, as that would expose their staff to someone with untreated presumed active TB. The only choice was to subject my very frail grandmother to a harsh 6-9 month TB treatment regimen with toxic side effects to the kidneys, liver and eyes that would likely hasten her death from organ failure rather than the suspected TB itself.

Part of TB treatment required obtaining samples for TB testing and culture for antimicrobial sensitivities before commencing empirical treatment. The process of even obtaining samples to test for TB was traumatic. With her dementia, the only way to get respiratory samples was through an invasive gastric lavage procedure - inserting a tube through a nostril, pharynx, oesophagus and into her stomach, leaving the NGT in-situ for multiple days to get 3 good quality early morning NG aspirates. Fortunately she was cooperative, and did not require repeated NGT insertions. The other option, sputum induction was not an option due to her dementia.

TB PCR result was negative. Acid-Fast Bacillus smear-positive. Culture is pending 8 weeks from time of collection.

There is no room for palliative, comfort-focused end-of-life care for suspected active TB patients in Singapore. Everyone has to undergo treatment, even if the family's goal is just to keep their terminally-ill loved one comfortable until the end. It forced me into an impossibly difficult decision that I felt was inhumane for my grandmother's condition.

I had to make the agonizing choice to pursue a path of treatment that would accelerate her decline and suffering rather than allow her a peaceful death from her terminal dementia. If only euthanasia were legal in Singapore, we could have spared her that traumatic final ordeal. It was an experience that made me wish deeply for the option of medical aid in dying for terminally ill patients suffering unbearably.

1.6 The Bare Minimum: Dealing with Doctors' Reluctance to Share Details

Engaging with most doctors in Singapore's public healthcare system is an uphill battle when it comes to open communication. It's akin to pulling teeth - in fact, I think an actual dental extraction would be more straightforward!

Whenever I ask an open-ended question, like inquiring about the full palliative care management plan, the responses I receive are woefully incomplete. Doctors tend to give bare-bones answers like "we just use subcutaneous fentanyl here." As someone with experience supporting palliative patients, I know there has to be more to it than that. It's only after pressing them with follow-up questions, essentially prompting them like a low-performing AI language model, that I finally get more comprehensive explanations.

Alternatively, I've found that asking extremely targeted questions can sometimes yield better results. However, even then, most doctors seem reluctant to proactively provide thorough information. Unless I demonstrate prior knowledge and ask about specifics, they tend to volunteer only the bare minimum details.

Overall, having an open discussion and getting full transparency into care plans is an incredible challenge. Doctors are often closed off, requiring me to pry out every piece of information bit-by-bit. As a patient or family member simply trying to understand and participate in care decisions, this lack of communication is extremely frustrating. Improving doctor-patient dialogue should be a priority for enhancing Singapore's healthcare experience.
