Section 1. Becoming a doctor

When I got my A level results, I was surprised. I guess I never thought I had options to dream about traditional prestigious careers. So I was quite unprepared for all the extra-curricular requirements and aptitude tests involved in dentistry college applications. By now, you would know the ending was that I was never successful in getting into dentistry.

Why dentistry? Because I would be considered a specialist within 4 years, while medical school was 5 years, and at the end of it, you are still considered the bottom of a tall hierarchy ladder. And as a dentist, the moment you graduate, your work life will always be reasonable, unlike a junior doctor.

I ended up studying pharmacy overseas, because I couldn't qualify for dentistry and pharmacy in Singapore, and I turned down TCM because using Mandarin in my training was too daunting for me.

I didn't consider applying for medicine at first because of my fear of gore. Something that I thought was a real fear because of the exposure to freddy kruger shows in my youth. That turned out not to be a problem because assisting in surgery turned out to be quite pleasant experience. With all the draping and taping, and only the surgical site exposed, the whole body parts and bloodiness felt rather sanitary and normal. All you could think of was that this patient needs help, and you are doing something that could hopefully fix the problem.

Funny enough, I should have known that my mind couldn't settle on being a pharmacist, so I applied to transfer to medicine in the university I was already studying in. And with AUD$35k downpayment, it was too late to regret the decision. I had already wasted too much of my parents' money to question my decision. I had to graduate as a doctor.

Section 2. Returning to Singapore

As an optimistic young doctor who has experience working in Australia, I was excited to contribute my knowledge and work style to Singapore's public healthcare system. When I started my rotation in General Medicine at Khoo Teck Puat Hospital, I expected a similar experience to my enjoyable times in Australia, where I learned from patients, seniors, peers, and allied health professionals.

However, I quickly faced a culture shock. Instead of a welcoming environment, I encountered a lack of open communication and mutual respect. Most consultants were unreceptive to my questions and input, leaving no room for clarification or discussion regarding their decisions. The hierarchical divide between junior and senior doctors was stark, and the divide between doctors and allied health professionals was even more concerning.

Allied health professionals were expected to treat doctors as infallible authorities, carrying out orders without question or offering their expertise. When I sought their opinions on patient care, they looked at me with bewilderment. Pharmacists were surprised when I asked for their input on medication regimens tailored to patients' unique physiology and presenting problems. Their response was often, "You're the doctor, shouldn't you already know this?"

It felt like a dystopian world where allied health professionals had lost their sense of ownership and pride in their roles. Their reply would typically be, "No opinion, nothing to say." Sadly, their valuable perspectives were suppressed, resulting in a loss for patients who could have benefited from the collective expertise of the healthcare team.

I firmly believe that open communication, mutual respect, and collaborative decision-making are essential for providing the best possible care. As healthcare professionals, we should embrace each other's unique specialties and foster an environment where diverse perspectives are valued and encouraged.

Section 3. Why I left the public healthcare system

The work culture shock and other darker reasons elaborated here were push factors for my leaving the frontline healthcare work option. Pull factors for my current line of work was the hope to eventually have an influence in the culture from another angle i.e. with technology.

I had always wondered why some EMR systems seem to be a joy to work with, while others were painful. Why some simple functions I envisioned as a user could be easily implemented were never implemented, or implemented unreliably. Now that I am on the other side, I finally understand why. One of the major contributing blockers is a mismatch and misunderstanding in priorities and method of implementation between technical staff and clinical staff.

I am reminded of this everytime I start work in the morning. It is my motivation for the choice to pursue a path less taken than most people with a medical degree. It is not to become venture capitalist, or a founder of startups, or a glorified chief executive or board member or medical sales liaison but to be intimately involved in digital solutions to improve the work lives of my fellow ex-colleagues, and hopefully also ensure safer and cost effective healthcare service delivery for my fellow residents in Singapore.
