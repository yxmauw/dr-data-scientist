Section 1. Becoming a doctor

When I got my A level results, I was surprised. I guess I never thought I had options to dream about traditional prestigious careers. So I was quite unprepared for all the extra-curricular requirements and aptitude tests involved in dentistry college applications. By now, you would know the ending was that I was never successful in getting into dentistry.

Why dentistry? Because I would be considered a specialist within 4 years, while medical school was 5 years, and at the end of it, you are still considered the bottom of a tall hierarchy ladder. And as a dentist, the moment you graduate, your work life will always be reasonable, unlike a junior doctor.

I ended up studying pharmacy overseas, because I couldn't qualify for dentistry and pharmacy in Singapore (didn't try apply for med), and I turned down TCM because using Mandarin in my training was too daunting for me.

I initially hesitated to apply for medical school due to my fear of gore, which I believed stemmed from exposure to Freddy Krueger films in my youth.

Side note: This concern proved unfounded when I discovered that assisting in surgery was actually quite a pleasant experience. With all the draping and taping, and only the surgical site exposed, the procedure felt surprisingly sanitary and normal. The focus on specific body parts and the controlled environment minimized any sense of bloodiness. In those moments, my only thought was that the patient needed help, and I was participating in something that could potentially solve their problem.

I enjoyed pharmacy well enough, learnt the art of compounding medications in various forms. It's not easy! Its like cooking, but no room for error. Naive me then felt my prospects were limited as a pharmacist. (Back then, I was still quite homesick, and I had this assumption that in Singapore, only pharmacists with good academic scores get to work in hospitals, everyone else had to settle for community retail stores.) Now I know after years of working, community retail pharmacy can be great for work-life harmony.

So I decided that since I was coming back to Singapore to work straight after graduation, I should maximise my prospects by switching to medicine.

(If you knew me well enough you would know that by the 4th year living in Australia, I fell in love with the country, and decided to stay and work. But life had other plans which eventually resulted me returning back to Singapore)

I applied to transfer to medicine in the university I was already studying in. And with AUD$35k downpayment, it was too late to regret the decision. I had already wasted too much of my parents' money to question my decision. I had to graduate as a doctor.

Section 2. Returning to Singapore

As an optimistic young doctor who has experience working in Australia, I was excited to contribute my knowledge and work style to Singapore's public healthcare system. When I started my rotation in General Medicine at Khoo Teck Puat Hospital, I expected a similar experience to my enjoyable times in Australia, where I learned from patients, seniors, peers, and allied health professionals.

However, I quickly faced a culture shock. Instead of a welcoming environment, I encountered a lack of open communication and mutual respect. Most consultants were unreceptive to my questions and input, leaving no room for clarification or discussion regarding their decisions. The hierarchical divide between junior and senior doctors was stark, and the divide between doctors and allied health professionals was even more concerning.

Allied health professionals were expected to treat doctors as infallible authorities, carrying out orders without question or offering their expertise. When I sought their opinions on patient care, they looked at me with bewilderment. Pharmacists were surprised when I asked for their input on medication regimens tailored to patients' unique physiology and presenting problems. Their response was often, "You're the doctor, shouldn't you already know this?"

It felt like a dystopian world where allied health professionals had lost their sense of ownership and pride in their roles. Their reply would typically be, "No opinion, nothing to say." Sadly, their valuable perspectives were suppressed, resulting in a loss for patients who could have benefited from the collective expertise of the healthcare team.

I firmly believe that open communication, mutual respect, and collaborative decision-making are essential for providing the best possible care. As healthcare professionals, we should embrace each other's unique specialties and foster an environment where diverse perspectives are valued and encouraged.

Section 3. Why I left the public healthcare system

The work culture shock and other darker reasons elaborated here were push factors for my leaving the frontline healthcare work option. Pull factors for my current line of work was the hope to eventually have an influence in the culture from another angle i.e. with technology.

I had always wondered why some EMR systems could be a joy to work with, while others were painful. Why some simple features I envisioned as a user could be easily implemented were never implemented, or implemented unreliably. Now that I am on the other side, I finally understand why. One of the major contributing blockers is a mismatch and misunderstanding in priorities and method of implementation between technical staff and clinical staff.

I am reminded of this everytime I start work in the morning. It is my motivation for the choice to pursue a path less taken than most people with a medical degree. It is not to become venture capitalist, or a founder of startups, or a glorified chief executive or board member or medical sales liaison but to be intimately involved in digital solutions to improve the work lives of my fellow ex-colleagues, and hopefully also ensure safer and cost effective healthcare service delivery for my fellow residents in Singapore.

Section 4. Transferring medical skills to data science

Now as a machine learning engineer, I have realized that many of my skills as a medical doctor transfer well to a career in data science. My analytical thinking, attention to detail, and ability to interpret complex information have been applicable. I'm accustomed to analyzing patient data, recognizing patterns, and making evidence-based decisions - all crucial skills in data science.

The scientific method and research skills I have honed in medicine translate effectively to the data science workflow. I have been trained to form hypotheses, design studies, collect and analyze data, and draw conclusions - a process very similar to the data science approach. Additionally, my experience with statistical analysis and interpreting research papers provides a solid foundation for more advanced data science concepts.

While transitioning to data science had required me to learn new technical skills like programming and machine learning algorithms, my existing knowledge of healthcare systems and medical terminology were particularly valuable in my current healthcare-related data science role. My domain expertise allows me to ask relevant questions, understand the context of the data, and derive meaningful insights in this field.

I can easily communicate with healthcare professionals and understand the context of their product feature requests without needing extensive elaboration. I can identify which features are critical to get right and which can be modified to work around technical and infrastructure limitations while still empowering healthcare providers to be more effective in fulfilling their responsibilities.

Section 5. Parallels between medicine and data science

Both fields rely heavily on pattern recognition. In medicine, doctors analyze symptoms, lab results, and medical histories to identify diseases. Similarly, data scientists examine large datasets to uncover trends and insights. This skill of spotting meaningful patterns amidst complex information is crucial in both domains.

Evidence-based decision making is fundamental to both medicine and data science. Doctors use clinical studies and established guidelines to inform treatment decisions. Data scientists similarly base their conclusions on statistical evidence and empirical data. Both professions require a rigorous approach to interpreting information and drawing actionable conclusions.

The scientific method underpins both disciplines. In medicine, hypotheses about diseases or treatments are tested through clinical trials. In data science, hypotheses about data relationships or predictive models are tested through statistical analysis and machine learning experiments. This systematic approach to problem-solving is a core similarity between the two fields.

Continuous learning and adaptation are essential in both medicine and data science. Medical knowledge evolves rapidly with new research, requiring doctors to stay updated. Similarly, data science techniques and technologies are constantly advancing, demanding ongoing education from practitioners. This shared need for lifelong learning reflects the dynamic nature of both fields.

Section 6. Vision for Health Tech future

My vision for the future of healthcare technology is one of seamless integration and personalization. I see a world where patient data flows effortlessly between devices and systems, creating a comprehensive health profile that evolves in real-time. This interconnected ecosystem would enable more accurate diagnoses, personalized treatment plans, and proactive health management.

Artificial intelligence will play a crucial role in this future. AI-powered diagnostic tools will assist healthcare providers, analyzing vast amounts of data to identify patterns and potential issues before they become serious. These systems will not replace human expertise but augment it, allowing healthcare professionals to focus more on patient care and complex decision-making.

6.1 AI in wearables and IoMT

Wearable technology and IoMT devices will become more sophisticated, continuously monitoring vital signs and other health indicators. This constant stream of data will feed into predictive models, alerting individuals and their healthcare providers to potential health risks before symptoms appear. This shift towards preventive care could dramatically reduce the burden on healthcare systems and improve overall public health.

6.2 Advanced Telemedicine

Telemedicine will evolve beyond simple video consultations, incorporating virtual and augmented reality to enable remote physical examinations and even surgical assistance. This will greatly improve access to specialized care, particularly for those in rural or underserved areas.

6.3 Treatment personalisation

Digital twins will revolutionize personalized medicine and treatment planning. Digital twins can be used to visualise virtual replicas of patients' physiological systems will allow healthcare providers to simulate and predict how individuals might respond to various treatments or interventions. By running countless scenarios on these digital models, we will be able to identify the most effective and least risky treatment options for each patient.

6.4 Preventive healthcare for the individual

Digital twins integrated with up-to-date population attributes and health statistics can also play a crucial role in preventive care, allowing us to model the long-term impacts of lifestyle choices and potential health risks. This technology will empower patients to make more informed decisions about their health and enable healthcare providers to deliver truly personalized care at an unprecedented level of precision.

6.5 Cost optimisation in healthcare institutions

Closer to personal experience, digital twins of healthcare institutions can revolutionize operational efficiency and patient safety. These virtual replicas can simulate various scenarios to optimize staff scheduling, equipment utilization, and patient flow, reducing operating costs and wait times.

They can monitor and predict supply chain needs in real-time, preventing shortages and minimizing waste. For infection control, digital twins can track the movement of people and equipment, simulating the spread of hospital-acquired infections and identifying high-risk areas or procedures.

This allows for proactive intervention strategies, such as adjusting cleaning protocols or patient placement. By providing a holistic view of the entire healthcare facility, digital twins enable data-driven decision-making that improves resource allocation, enhances patient care, and ultimately leads to better health outcomes while controlling costs.

6.6 Safer and faster drug discovery

In the future, AI-driven drug and protein discovery could revolutionize personalized medicine for cancer, congenital, and autoimmune diseases. Advanced machine learning algorithms will analyze vast datasets of genetic information, protein structures, and patient outcomes to design highly targeted therapies. These treatments will be tailored to an individual's unique genetic makeup and disease profile, maximizing efficacy while minimizing side effects.

For cancer, this might mean developing drugs that precisely target tumor cells without harming healthy tissue. In congenital diseases, we could see gene therapies that correct specific mutations with pinpoint accuracy. For autoimmune conditions, AI could help create treatments that modulate the immune system without compromising its overall function.

The combination of AI-driven research and advanced manufacturing techniques like 3D printing of drugs could dramatically reduce development and production costs once they become more mainstream technologies. This could make curative, side-effect-free treatments accessible to the general population, potentially through government-subsidized programs or new insurance models designed to cover these possibly long-term cost effective but higher upfront cost therapies.

The result would be a healthcare landscape where chronic diseases become manageable or even curable conditions, significantly improving quality of life and reducing the long-term burden on healthcare systems.
